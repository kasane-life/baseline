Up and running in 2 minutes

01

Add one line to Claude

Add the MCP server to your Claude Desktop config. That's the only install step.

02

Say "set me up"

Claude walks you through it. Age and sex are the only required fields. Everything else is optional. The engine scores whatever you give it and shows you the gaps.

03

Ask "how am I doing?"

Get your first score immediately. Then add data over time. Log a weight, connect Garmin or import Apple Health, drop in lab results. Your coverage grows and the coaching gets sharper.

# Add to Claude Desktop config (~/.claude/mcp.json)
{
  "mcpServers": {
    "health-engine": {
      "command": "uvx",
      "args": ["health-engine"]
    }
  }
}

# Restart Claude. Ask: "set me up" or "how am I doing?"
# That's it. No cloning, no pip install, no config files.

Requires Python 3.11+ and uv installed. Or clone from GitHub if you want to hack on it.

What makes this different

→
Clinical zones, not just ranges. "Normal" on a lab report means "not immediately sick." We score you as Optimal, Healthy, Borderline, or Elevated using the same thresholds your cardiologist uses to make treatment decisions.
→
Freshness decay. A lipid panel from 18 months ago isn't worth as much as one from last month. Data decays based on biological variation. Wearable data expires in weeks. Lp(a) never expires (it's genetic).
→
Cross-metric patterns. Individual numbers tell part of the story. When your triglycerides are high, HDL is low, and insulin is creeping up, that's a pattern. The engine detects these compound signals automatically because they matter more than any single number.
→
Reliability multipliers. A single blood pressure reading is noisy (0.5x credit). A 7-day protocol is definitive (1.0x). Single hs-CRP readings get 0.6x because it's too variable. The engine knows when to trust data and when to ask for more.
→
Private by default. Your health data lives on your machine. The engine runs on your laptop. No data is uploaded, no accounts, no cloud sync, no third-party access. You own it.

Connect what you have

Garmin, Apple Health, or nothing. The engine works with whatever data exists. Labs give you the clinical picture. Wearables give you the daily signal. Together they're more complete than either alone.

→

→
Apple Health. Export from your iPhone, import the file. Handles years of data. Same metrics, same scoring.
→
No wearable? That's fine. The engine scores whatever you give it. Even just a $30 Lp(a) test and a $40 blood pressure cuff gets you into the most important metrics.

About me

I'm Andrew Deal. Former engineer and product lead. Shipped products across gaming, ag, defense, construction, and graphic design. Spent my 20s running a gym. Now a health and wellness entrepreneur building the tools I wish existed when I was training clients.

Somewhere in my 30s I realized how little most people actually understand about their own health data. You get your labs back. Your doctor says "everything looks normal" in a 7-minute appointment. Your wearable shows pretty charts with no context. Meanwhile, 96 million Americans are prediabetic. Half don't know it. And "normal" lab ranges are just the middle 95% of a sick population.

I started building this for myself. I wanted to know what my cholesterol particles, fasting insulin, and heart rate variability actually meant. Not in isolation, but together, scored against real population data, with the same thresholds cardiologists use. Then I realized: everyone deserves this. Not just people who can afford a $3,000/year concierge doctor or a $500 Function Health panel.

Lately I've been going deep with Paul Mederos, who's building Kasane, a daily wellness app focused on habits and behavior change. We're figuring out how scoring and daily practice fit together. Stay tuned.

The data is yours. The research is public. The engine should be too. The whole thing is open source. The methodology is transparent. Every scoring decision is documented and cited. If I got something wrong, you can see exactly where and tell me.
