Not another dashboard

You don't need more charts. You need someone who's read the research, looked at your numbers, and can tell you what actually matters right now.

Health Engine scores you using clinical thresholds from the AHA, ADA, and ESC — the same cutoffs your cardiologist uses. Then it layers in population percentiles from NHANES (300K+ Americans) so you know where you stand relative to everyone else.

The difference: the 50th percentile is the median American — 42% obese, 38% prediabetic. "Better than average" is a low bar. Clinical zones tell you if you're actually healthy. Population data tells you how rare that is.

Up and running in 2 minutes

01

Add one line to Claude

Add the MCP server to your Claude Desktop config. That's the only install step.

02

Say "set me up"

Claude walks you through it. Age and sex are the only required fields. Everything else is optional — the engine scores whatever you give it and shows you the gaps.

03

Ask "how am I doing?"

Get your first score immediately. Then add data over time — log a weight, connect Garmin or import Apple Health, drop in lab results. Your coverage grows. The coaching gets sharper.

# Add to Claude Desktop config (~/.claude/mcp.json)
{
  "mcpServers": {
    "health-engine": {
      "command": "uvx",
      "args": ["health-engine"]
    }
  }
}

# Restart Claude. Ask: "set me up" or "how am I doing?"
# That's it. No cloning, no pip install, no config files.

Requires Python 3.11+ and uv installed. Or clone from GitHub if you want to hack on it.

What makes this different

→
Clinical zones, not just ranges. "Normal" on a lab report means "not immediately sick." We use Optimal / Healthy / Borderline / Elevated thresholds from AHA, ADA, and ESC — the same cutoffs that inform treatment decisions.
→
Freshness decay. A lipid panel from 18 months ago isn't worth as much as one from last month. Data decays based on biological variation — wearable data expires in weeks, Lp(a) never expires (it's genetic).
→
Cross-metric patterns. Metabolic syndrome detection (3+ of 5 criteria). Atherogenic dyslipidemia (TG/HDL ratio). Insulin resistance patterns. Recovery stress signals from wearable data. The compound signal is more important than any single number.
→
Reliability multipliers. A single blood pressure reading is noisy (0.5x credit). A 7-day protocol is definitive (1.0x). Single hs-CRP readings get 0.6x — it's too variable. The engine knows when to trust data and when to ask for more.
→
Local-first. Always. Your health data lives on your machine. The engine runs locally. Claude connects via MCP — no data leaves your laptop. No accounts, no cloud sync, no third-party access.

Connect what you have

Garmin, Apple Health, or nothing — the engine works with whatever data exists. Labs give you the clinical picture. Wearables give you the daily signal. Together they're a more complete picture than either alone.

→
Garmin Connect — authenticate once via CLI, tokens cached. Pulls RHR, HRV, sleep, steps, VO2 max, zone 2, workouts.
→
Apple Health — export from your iPhone, import the ZIP. SAX streaming handles the massive XML files. Same metrics, same scoring.
→
No wearable? That's fine. The engine scores whatever you give it — even just a $30 Lp(a) test and a $40 blood pressure cuff gets you into the highest-leverage metrics.

About me

I'm Andrew Deal. Former engineer and product lead — shipped products across gaming, ag, defense, construction, and graphic design. Spent my 20s running a gym. Now a health and wellness entrepreneur building the tools I wish existed when I was training clients.

Somewhere in my 30s I realized how little most people actually understand about their own health data. You get your labs back. Your doctor says "everything looks normal" in a 7-minute appointment. Your wearable shows pretty charts with no context. Meanwhile, 96 million Americans are prediabetic — half don't know it — and "normal" lab ranges are just the middle 95% of a sick population.

I started building this for myself. I wanted to know what my ApoB, fasting insulin, and HRV actually meant — not in isolation, but together, against real population data, with clinical thresholds from the research that matters. Then I realized: everyone deserves this. Not just people who can afford a $3,000/year concierge doctor or a $500 Function Health panel.

The data is yours. The research is public. The engine should be too. The whole thing is open source. The methodology is transparent. Every scoring decision is documented and cited. If I got something wrong, you can see exactly where and tell me.
